Doppelganger — Update for Villi Iltchev

Zobeir Hamid & Ahmad Jiha — April 30, 2026

TL;DR

We've named the product Doppelganger and run user interviews with non-technical knowledge workers across multiple fields. The thesis we set out to test holds, and is now sharper:

  • Demand is universal. Users consistently surface recurring workflows they want to automate but can't trust existing tools to handle.
  • The blocker is training, not setup. The "implicit knowledge gap" — agents lacking the context, edge cases, and judgment a coworker would absorb on the job — is the real wall.
  • The right starting customer is the ambitious IC, confirmed by users already paying out of pocket for Claude, Granola, and similar tools.
  • Land first in the low-stakes × high-frequency quadrant, where the training-by-correction loop compounds fastest and per-run blast radius is bounded.
  • New product principle: scoped agent identity. Users don't want agents acting as them with their credentials — they want a bounded coworker with its own email, integrations, and permissions.

Detail and reasoning below.

The Thesis Going In

Three connected hypotheses we needed to validate:

  1. Most non-technical knowledge workers have recurring workflows they would happily automate, if automation were trustworthy.
  2. The blocker is training, not infrastructure. Today's agents fail because they expect users to articulate, in a single prompt, the implicit context a coworker would absorb over months on the job.
  3. The right starting customer is the ambitious IC — they own the workflow, supervise the output, benefit personally from leverage, and pay out of pocket for visible effectiveness.

If any of these were wrong, the wedge collapses.

What the Interviews Validated

Latent demand is universal. Without exception, users described recurring workflows they want off their plate but cannot delegate to today's AI tools.

The implicit-knowledge gap is real, and now quotable. One user tried to automate a recurring multi-step Zoom-to-Dropbox workflow with Zapier. It failed silently mid-run because the file size exceeded a Dropbox limit:

"I told it it was a webinar, so it should have inferred the file size would exceed Dropbox's limit."

She gave the agent context, assumed coworker-level inference of the implied constraint, and lost trust when it didn't materialize. Variants of this pattern showed up throughout the cohort.

Trust failure is contagious. Distrust spreads — within a single user (one prior bad experience pre-poisons the next attempt) and across an org (one IC's failure makes the next IC's adoption harder). The product has to feel demonstrably different from generic Zapier-style automation on first contact, not just incrementally better.

The ambitious-IC persona is confirmed. Users pay for AI tools out of pocket despite employer-provided alternatives, or invest personal time specifically to look more effective at work. They are paying — in money or time — for visible leverage. Exactly the customer Doppelganger is built for.

The Conceptual Frame: Stakes × Frequency

The most useful framework that emerged:

Low frequency High frequency
High stakes Least appealing — risk dominates Trust must be earned slowly, with heavy supervision
Low stakes Moderate appeal — one-off logistics Golden quadrant — volume justifies setup, blast radius bounded

Why it's load-bearing for us:

  • It explains user behavior better than rhetoric does. One user called AI "a scam" out loud, but her actual delegation tracked stakes precisely: she refused agents on contracts and bank-related email, welcomed them on weekly recurring orders. Loud distrust often masks a precise, defensible stakes-based mental model.
  • It tells us where to land first. The bottom-right cell — low stakes, high frequency — is where the training-by-correction loop compounds fastest. We expect to climb the matrix as trust accumulates: into high-stakes/high-frequency over time, and only later toward high-stakes/low-frequency, if at all.

New Product Principle: Scoped Agent Identity

A finding sharp enough to elevate to a core principle. Users do not want agents acting as them with their credentials. One user was unwilling to grant an agent access to her email (where bank communications live) — but entirely comfortable if the agent had its own email.

This reframes credential and integration design from "OAuth into the user's life" to "stand the agent up as a separate principal." Scoped credentials, scoped permissions, agent-as-coworker by default.

Secondary Signals We're Tracking

  • One-time setup may itself be a wedge. Users closed on "live in my inbox forever" are sometimes open to "set up my funnel for me." Most agent products lean ambient; there may be an underserved opening in being explicitly excellent at heavy one-time setup, then earning recurrence.
  • Voice as context-capture, not chat. Users describe wanting to narrate implicit context rather than type it. Voice may be one mechanism for closing the implicit-knowledge gap without forcing users to formalize their mental model in writing.
  • Tool lock-in is social, not technical. Whether an IC pays out of pocket for their own tool or accepts the company default correlates with tenure and standing — open question worth probing.
  • "We could build this ourselves." The gap between could build and actually built, trained, and trusted in production is exactly where the product's value lives.

Where This Lands Us

  • Doppelganger is a platform for non-technical ICs to create, train, and deploy specialized agents on their recurring workflows.
  • The first workflows ship in the low-stakes, high-frequency quadrant.
  • Scoped agent identity is now a core product principle, not a future consideration.
  • The ambitious IC remains the right starting customer; bottom-up GTM motion is intact.

Next Steps

Running a second round of interviews aimed at the open questions above (tenure-to-opt-out, one-time-setup wedge, voice as context-capture), and locking the first specific workflow in the low-stakes/high-frequency quadrant with a small set of design partners.

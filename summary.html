Why We Started Doppelganger

We built an early version of this internally at Anything and were leading the effort. Non-technical staff there got excited enough about what specialized, trained agents could do for their workflows that the company pivoted its entire product direction around it within weeks. Since we were already leading it, we knew this should exist as its own company, not a feature inside someone else's. We left Anything, prototyped externally, and have been pressure-testing it with early users since.

The deeper reason: we believe agents are the rare application of AI that can materially improve people's lives now — by freeing knowledge workers to focus on the work they love and offloading the rest. But the version of that promise being shipped today doesn't actually work for most workers, and we know specifically why.

Why Knowledge Workers Can't Automate Their Own Workflows

Most knowledge workers have tedious, recurring workflows an agent could handle. The blocker isn't setup — it's training.

People think they've explained a task clearly because a human coworker would fill in the missing context, preferences, and edge cases. Agents don't. That gap is why most agent deployments fail to earn trust. The required knowledge is implicit: judgment a coworker absorbs over months on the job, encoded in no document. When users instruct an agent and the output is wrong, they don't have a vocabulary for what was missing — they just stop trusting it.

This is non-trivial and easy to verify: grab a non-technical person, set up their environment, and ask them to get an agent to do their actual job. You'll see it immediately.

Why the Ambitious Individual Contributor

Our target customer is the ambitious individual contributor at a mid-size or enterprise company — the IC trying to get promoted, hit numbers, or look superhuman to their manager. They already pay out of pocket for tools like ChatGPT Plus, Granola, or Superhuman because their job rewards visible output.

They're the right starting customer because their incentives line up with what an agent actually needs to be useful:

  • They own the workflow, so they know the implicit context.
  • They supervise the output, so they catch failures early.
  • They benefit directly from training the agent, so they'll do the work.
  • They'll pay out of pocket for the leverage, so we don't need procurement.

Top-down enterprise AI deployments stall on long sales cycles, unsolved security and permissions, and the change management required to roll AI out across a team. None of those slow down an individual using an agent on their own work. We can ship to ICs and earn revenue now, while the harder enterprise problems get solved over time.

What Is the Product?

A platform for non-technical users to create, train, and deploy specialized AI agents for specific repeatable workflows.

  • Create — users start from a workflow they already own (no CLI, no prompt engineering).
  • Train — they teach the agent through instructions, examples, corrections, and approvals on real work. Each correction sharpens the agent's playbook.
  • Deploy — the trained agent slots into the tools where work already happens, with the right integrations, permissions, and guardrails.

Unlike general-purpose assistants that try to learn your job from a single prompt, Doppelganger agents get sharper every time you use them. We've already shipped this stack at our previous startup, so we know firsthand where the training gap breaks adoption.

What Is the Business Model?

Self-serve individual subscription, priced so adoption doesn't require manager approval or procurement. As users cluster inside the same companies, we layer in team plans, then company-wide plans — converting bottom-up adoption into enterprise revenue the same way Notion, Figma, and Linear did, but without the multi-year enterprise sales cycles that have stalled top-down agent deployments.

How Big Is the Market?

Most jobs are non-technical. Inside the technical segment, agents already have wide penetration — basically every engineer uses AI tools now. In the non-technical segment it's the exact opposite: lots of talk and hype, relatively low penetration of agentic products that actually do work. That gap is the opportunity. Every ambitious knowledge worker who'd pay for leverage at work is in our addressable market.

What Is the GTM Strategy?

The product does most of the work. An agent that visibly makes someone superhuman at their job travels through their network without us pushing it.

We seed distribution through:

  • Content — concrete demos of agents doing real work.
  • Communities — where ambitious ICs already gather (Twitter/X, niche Slack and Discord communities, Substack newsletters in their field).
  • Referrals — from each user we win.

As users cluster inside the same companies, we add team plans, then company-wide plans. This is the path Notion, Figma, and Linear took: one ambitious IC at a time, until the company has no choice but to formalize what its best people are already doing.

Why Now?

Three converging shifts:

  1. Frontier models are finally good enough to do real multi-step work — but only if grounded properly. The harness, RAG, and prompt-routing infrastructure that makes agents reliable has matured (we built one version of it at Anything).
  2. Engineers got their agent revolution with Cursor and Claude Code. Everyone else is still waiting. The systems that made those products work for engineers can now be turned outward.
  3. Top-down enterprise agent deployments have stalled. Sales cycles, security, and permissions remain unsolved. The bottoms-up window is open now — and closes once incumbents figure out the IC motion.

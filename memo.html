Why Did We Start Doppelganger?

We had been talking about this for a while: agents are the application of AI that can materially improve lives now — freeing workers to focus on the parts of their job they love, rather than everything required to support those parts.

Then we built an internal beta at Anything and led it. Non-technical staff got so excited about specialized, trained agents for their workflows that Anything pivoted the entire company toward it within weeks. Since we were already leading it, that was the signal: this isn't a feature, it's a company — and it should be ours. We left, started talking to target users, and are now locking the first specific workflow and surfacing the hidden details that make agents fail in practice.

Why Now?

  1. Model + grounding maturity. Agent harnesses, RAG, model routing, and prompt caching have all crossed the reliability threshold. We built versions of each in production at Anything.
  2. Engineers already crossed over. Cursor and Claude Code proved the agent revolution works for users who can articulate what they want. Non-technical knowledge workers are next, but they need a fundamentally different product — one built around training, not prompting.
  3. Enterprise top-down has stalled. Sales cycles, security, and permissions are unsolved. The bottoms-up window is open and an ambitious IC will pay out of pocket today for the leverage.

What Is Our Vision?

Every knowledge worker has a roster of specialized agents — one per recurring workflow — that they trained themselves and trust to do real work. The agents live in the tools where work already happens. Companies don't issue agents top-down; their best people bring agents in, the way they brought Notion, Figma, and Linear in. Doppelganger is the platform underneath all of it.

What Is the Product?

A platform for non-technical users to create, train, and deploy specialized agents for specific repeatable workflows.

  • Creation — start from a workflow you already own. No CLI, no prompt engineering, no environment setup that requires an engineer.
  • Training — teach the agent through instructions, examples, corrections, and approvals on real work. Every correction sharpens the agent's internal playbook.
  • Deployment — the trained agent slots into the tools where work happens (email, docs, CRMs, internal apps) with the right integrations, permissions, and guardrails.
  • Compounding reliability — unlike a general-purpose assistant trying to infer your job from a single prompt, a Doppelganger agent gets sharper every time it runs.

Who Are We Trying to Help?

Ambitious individual contributors at mid-size and enterprise companies — ICs who already pay out of pocket for ChatGPT Plus, Granola, or Superhuman because their job rewards visible output. They own their workflows, supervise their own outputs, and benefit directly from making themselves more effective. Their incentives line up with what an agent needs to be useful, which is real training. That's what makes them the right starting customer.

How Do We Win the Market?

Bottoms-up, IC by IC. The product does the distribution: an agent that makes someone visibly superhuman at their job travels through their network without us pushing it.

  • Seed: content, demos of agents doing real work, communities where ambitious ICs gather, referrals from every user we win.
  • Price: self-serve individual subscription so adoption doesn't require manager approval or procurement.
  • Expand: as users cluster inside the same companies, we add team plans, then company-wide plans — the Notion / Figma / Linear path.

The bet: by the time enterprise sales cycles for top-down agent platforms close, we're already inside the company through its best people.

What Are the Challenges?

  • Crowded, well-funded category. Anthropic, OpenAI, Salesforce, and every horizontal AI player is moving toward agents. We have to be specifically the platform for non-technical training, not generically "an agent product."
  • Training UX is the make-or-break. If teaching the agent feels harder than doing the task, ICs churn. The product has to make corrections feel natural — not like prompt engineering.
  • Last-mile setup. Some non-technical users will still need help wiring permissions and integrations the first time. We need this to feel solvable, not blocking.
  • Bottoms-up revenue ramps slowly relative to enterprise contracts. Numbers will look small for the first 12–18 months; we need patient capital.
  • Trust failures compound. One wrong agent output on real work erodes trust faster than ten correct outputs build it. Reliability isn't a nice-to-have, it's the product.
